## Dissertation

My dissertation, "The Politics of Climate Adaptation," examines whether institutional adaptation affects individuals' engagement in risk-sensitive behaviors such as migration, and whether adaptation policies influence candidate electability. Original survey experiments with over 9,800 respondents recruited via social media in Indonesia.

**Committee:** Daniel Masterson (chair), Matto Mildenberger, Mark Buntaine

### Paper 1: Risk and Adaptation

Survey experiment (N=3,038) testing whether exposure to information about public climate adaptation projects shifts willingness to take risks. Vignette experiment with 5 treatment arms vs. control. Adaptation treatment increases risk-sensitive behavior by 0.08 SD (p=0.016). *Analysis complete, manuscript in progress.*

### Paper 2: Climate Promises and Voter Choice

Three embedded survey experiments (N=6,773): candidate conjoint, policy conjoint, and corruption vignette. Climate adaptation promises do not significantly increase candidate support overall, but disaster experience moderates the effect: low-experience voters penalize climate promises while high-experience voters are neutral. *Analysis in progress.*

### Paper 3: Partisan Alignment and Climate Adaptation Allocation

How does partisan alignment between local and national governments affect the allocation of climate adaptation resources? Using subnational fiscal and administrative data from Indonesia, this paper examines whether politically aligned districts receive more adaptation funding and programming. *In progress.*
